Poem 1: The Library

What do these lines mean?

These lines mean that the library looks like any building we pass along in a street.

Paraphrase (pum;ajy):

"It looks like any building When you pass it on the street": This line means that the library looks like any other ordinary building we pass by on the street.

"Made of stone and glass and marble, Made of iron and concrete": This line means that the library is built with materials like stone, glass, marble, iron, and concrete.

"But once inside you can ride a camel or a train": Inside the library, you can imagine yourself riding a camel or a train through reading.

"Visit Rome, Siam, or Nome. Feel a hurricane": Inside the library, you can also imagine visiting different places like Rome, Siam, or Nome, or experiencing a hurricane.

"Meet a king, learn to sing How to bake a pie": The library lets you imagine meeting important people or learning new skills like singing and baking.

"Go to sea, plant a tree. Find how airplanes fly": You can imagine going to the sea, planting a tree, or learning how airplanes fly.

"Train a horse, and of course Have all the dogs you'd like": Inside the library, you can imagine learning to train horses and having as many dogs as you'd like.

"See the moon, a sandy dune, Or catch a whopping pike": You can imagine seeing the moon, exploring dunes, or catching a big fish.

"Everything that books can bring You'll find inside those walls": Everything that books offer can be found inside the library.

"A world is there for you to share When adventure calls": The library offers a world of adventure for you to explore and share.

"You cannot tell its magic By the way the building looks": You cannot judge the library's magic just by looking at its outside appearance.

"But there's wonderment within it. The wonderment of books": The true wonder of the library lies inside, where books offer endless opportunities for learning and imagination.

Poem 2: The Quiet Life

What do these lines mean?

These lines mean that a happy man is one whose desires are simple and limited to what he has inherited.

Paraphrase (pum;ajy):

"Happy the man, whose wish and care A few paternal acres bound": A happy man desires only simple things, such as a small piece of land passed down from his ancestors.

"Content to breathe his native air In his own ground": He is satisfied living in his homeland, breathing the air of his own land.

"Whose herds with milk, whose fields with bread, Whose flocks supply him with attire": His herds provide him with milk, his fields with bread, and his flocks supply him with clothes.

"Whose trees in summer yield him shade, In winter, fire": His trees provide him shade in summer and firewood in winter for warmth.

"Blest, who can unconcern’dly find Hours, days and years, slide soft away": He is blessed to live without worry, letting time pass peacefully and quietly.

"In health of body, peace of mind, Quiet by day": He is in good health, with peace of mind, living a quiet life.

"Sound sleep by night; study and ease Together mix’d; sweet recreation": He enjoys restful sleep at night and balances work and relaxation during the day.

"And innocence, which most does please With meditation": A happy man enjoys the peaceful innocence of his life, reflected in his thoughts and meditation.

"Thus let me live, unseen, unknown; Thus unlamented let me die": The poet desires to live a simple, quiet life without fame or attention, and to die without anyone mourning him.

"Steal from the world, and not a stone Tell where I lie": He wishes to leave quietly, with no stone marking his grave.
